# Hemivertebra

## Definition

A hemivertebra is a congenital anomaly of vertebral formation that results from unilateral failure of half of the vertebral body to form. This results in a wedge-shaped vertebral body that produces lateral curvature (congenital scoliosis) or kyphosis depending on its location and whether it is segmented or unsegmented.

## Classification

### By Segmentation
- **Fully segmented** — Normal disc spaces above and below the hemivertebra. This type has the highest growth potential and is most likely to cause progressive deformity.
- **Semi-segmented** — A disc space on one side but fusion to an adjacent vertebra on the other side. Moderate growth potential.
- **Non-segmented (incarcerated)** — Fused to the vertebrae above and below on both sides. Limited growth potential — least likely to cause progressive deformity.

### By Location
- **Lateral** — Produces congenital scoliosis
- **Posterior** — Produces congenital kyphosis (more dangerous because of potential for cord compression)
- **Posterolateral** — Produces kyphoscoliosis

## Imaging Findings

### Radiography/CT
- Wedge-shaped vertebral body with a contralateral body deficiency
- Associated curvature (scoliosis or kyphosis)
- Evaluate the disc spaces above and below to classify segmentation status
- May be associated with contralateral bar (unsegmented bar + hemivertebra is the worst combination for progressive deformity)

### MRI
- Evaluates the spinal cord for associated anomalies (diastematomyelia, tethered cord, syringomyelia)
- Cord compression from kyphotic hemivertebra
- All patients with congenital scoliosis should have MRI to exclude intraspinal anomalies before surgical correction

!!! tip "Clinical Pearl"
    The combination of a **contralateral unsegmented bar + hemivertebra** produces the most rapidly progressive congenital scoliosis because the bar prevents growth on one side while the hemivertebra drives growth on the other. All patients with congenital vertebral anomalies should have MRI of the entire spine to evaluate for associated spinal cord anomalies (present in up to 25%).

## Key Points

- Congenital anomaly of vertebral formation — only one half develops
- Fully segmented hemivertebrae have the highest growth potential
- Associated spinal cord anomalies in up to 25% — MRI is mandatory
- Contralateral bar + hemivertebra is the worst combination for progressive deformity
- Posterior hemivertebra causing kyphosis is more dangerous than lateral (scoliosis)
