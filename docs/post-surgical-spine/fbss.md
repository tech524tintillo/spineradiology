# Failed Back Surgery Syndrome

## Definition

Failed back surgery syndrome (FBSS) is persistent or recurrent pain after spinal surgery. It is not a single diagnosis but a clinical syndrome with multiple potential causes, requiring systematic imaging evaluation to identify the specific etiology.

## Causes

### Recurrent/Residual Pathology
- Recurrent disc herniation at the operative level
- Residual disc fragment (inadequate decompression)
- Residual or recurrent spinal stenosis

### Post-Surgical Complications
- Epidural fibrosis (scar tissue) compressing nerve roots
- Post-surgical instability or spondylolisthesis
- Adjacent segment disease
- Pseudarthrosis (nonunion)
- Hardware failure (loosening, breakage, malposition)
- Recurrent herniation at a new level

### Non-Surgical Causes
- Sacroiliac joint dysfunction
- Facet arthropathy
- Myofascial pain
- Arachnoiditis (clumping of cauda equina nerve roots)
- Psychological factors

## Imaging Approach

1. **Radiography** — Alignment, hardware position, instability (flexion-extension)
2. **MRI with contrast** — Distinguish scar from recurrent disc, evaluate for stenosis, cord/root compression, arachnoiditis
3. **CT** — Hardware assessment, fusion status, bony stenosis

!!! tip "Clinical Pearl"
    A systematic approach to FBSS imaging: (1) Is the surgery adequate? — check for residual stenosis or disc fragment. (2) Is there a new problem? — recurrent herniation, adjacent segment disease, instability. (3) Is there a complication? — pseudarthrosis, hardware failure, epidural scar. (4) Is the pain source non-spinal? — SI joint, facet, myofascial.

## Key Points

- FBSS is a syndrome, not a single diagnosis — systematic evaluation is essential
- Post-contrast MRI is the key study for soft tissue evaluation
- Recurrent disc vs scar is the most common diagnostic question
- Always evaluate hardware position, fusion status, and adjacent segments
- Non-surgical pain generators should be considered
