# Disc Protrusion

## Definition

A disc protrusion is a type of herniation in which the base of the displaced disc material at the parent disc is **wider** than the herniated portion extending beyond the base. The outer annular fibers may be intact (contained) or disrupted, but the overall morphology shows a broad-based, dome-shaped extension.

## Imaging Findings

### MRI

- Focal disc extension beyond the vertebral margin — the base at the disc level is wider than the apex
- Signal usually similar to the parent disc (intermediate T1, variable T2 depending on hydration)
- Best seen on **axial T2** images for precise characterization
- Sagittal T2 shows the craniocaudal extent
- May indent the thecal sac or contact nerve roots without displacing them

### Key Measurements

- Mild: <25% of the spinal canal AP diameter
- Moderate: 25–50%
- Severe: >50%

!!! tip "Clinical Pearl"
    Disc protrusions are the **most common type of herniation** and are frequently asymptomatic. The clinical significance depends on the location relative to neural structures and the presence of nerve root contact, displacement, or compression — not merely the presence of the protrusion itself. A small paracentral protrusion contacting the traversing nerve root may cause significant radiculopathy, while a larger central protrusion may be entirely asymptomatic.

## Key Points

- A protrusion has a base wider than the extruded dome — "broad-based" herniation
- Most common herniation subtype; frequently incidental
- Clinical significance depends on location and degree of neural compression
- Best characterized on axial T2 MRI
