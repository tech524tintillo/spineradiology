# Occipital Condyle Fracture

## Definition

Occipital condyle fractures are injuries to the bony prominences at the base of the skull that articulate with the lateral masses of the atlas (C1) to form the atlanto-occipital joints. These fractures are frequently associated with high-energy trauma and were historically underdiagnosed before the widespread use of CT scanning.

## Classification — Anderson and Montesano

**Type I — Impaction Fracture**
Comminuted fracture of the occipital condyle resulting from axial loading. The alar ligament and tectorial membrane remain intact. This is a stable injury.

**Type II — Basilar Skull Fracture Extension**
A fracture line extends from the basilar skull into one or both occipital condyles. This is typically a stable injury as the alar ligament remains intact.

**Type III — Avulsion Fracture**
An avulsion fracture of the medial aspect of the occipital condyle at the attachment site of the alar ligament. This is potentially unstable because the alar ligament — a critical stabilizer of the craniocervical junction — is disrupted.

## Mechanism of Injury

Occipital condyle fractures result from high-energy mechanisms including motor vehicle collisions, falls from height, and axial loading injuries. Type I fractures are caused by axial compression, Type II by direct trauma, and Type III by rotation or lateral bending with distraction.

## Imaging Findings

### CT
- Thin-section CT with coronal and sagittal reformats is essential for diagnosis
- Fracture lines through the occipital condyle, which may be unilateral or bilateral
- Type I shows comminution without significant displacement
- Type II shows a fracture line extending from the adjacent skull base
- Type III shows an avulsed bone fragment from the inferomedial condyle

### MRI
- Evaluates alar ligament integrity (critical for stability assessment)
- High T2/STIR signal around the craniocervical junction indicates edema and potential ligamentous injury
- May demonstrate associated atlanto-occipital dissociation

!!! tip "Clinical Pearl"
    Bilateral occipital condyle fractures and Type III avulsion fractures should raise concern for craniocervical instability and potential atlanto-occipital dissociation. CT angiography should be considered to evaluate the vertebral arteries, which are in close proximity to the occipital condyles.

## Associated Injuries

- Atlanto-occipital dissociation
- Atlas (C1) fractures
- Cranial nerve palsies (particularly CN IX, X, XI, XII — the lower cranial nerves exit through the jugular foramen and hypoglossal canal adjacent to the occipital condyles)
- Vertebral artery injury

## Management

Type I and Type II fractures are typically treated conservatively with a rigid cervical collar. Type III fractures may require halo vest immobilization or surgical fixation if there is evidence of craniocervical instability.

## Key Points

- Occipital condyle fractures are best detected on thin-section CT with multiplanar reformats
- Anderson and Montesano Type III (alar ligament avulsion) is the only potentially unstable type
- Bilateral condyle fractures should raise suspicion for craniocervical instability
- MRI assesses alar ligament integrity and associated soft tissue injury
- CT angiography should be considered to evaluate the vertebral arteries
