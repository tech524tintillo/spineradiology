# Tarlov Cysts

## Definition

Tarlov cysts (perineural cysts) are CSF-filled cysts arising from the nerve root sheaths, most commonly in the sacral spine (S2–S3). They are very common — found in approximately 5–9% of spine MRIs — and are almost always incidental and asymptomatic.

## Imaging Findings

### MRI
- CSF-signal cysts (T1 dark, T2 bright) in the sacral spinal canal, arising from the nerve root sleeves
- The nerve root fibers may be seen traversing the cyst wall or within the cyst
- Can be unilateral or bilateral, single or multiple
- May cause sacral bone remodeling (scalloping) when large

### CT Myelography
- Delayed filling with contrast (hours after intrathecal injection) — the cyst communicates with the subarachnoid space through a narrow neck (ball-valve mechanism)
- This delayed filling distinguishes Tarlov cysts from simple meningeal diverticula

!!! tip "Clinical Pearl"
    Tarlov cysts are almost always **incidental findings** that should NOT be attributed as the cause of low back pain or radiculopathy without exhaustive exclusion of other causes. Large cysts (>1.5 cm) occasionally cause symptoms from mass effect on adjacent nerve roots, but this is rare. Over-attributing symptoms to Tarlov cysts leads to unnecessary interventions.

## Key Points

- CSF-signal sacral perineural cysts — very common incidental finding (5–9%)
- Almost always asymptomatic — do not attribute symptoms without excluding other causes
- Nerve root fibers within or on the cyst wall distinguish from simple meningeal cysts
- Large cysts may remodel sacral bone
- Delayed filling on CT myelography is characteristic
