# Hyperparathyroidism

## Definition

Hyperparathyroidism causes excessive parathyroid hormone (PTH) secretion, leading to increased osteoclastic bone resorption. In the spine, it produces osteopenia, subchondral resorption, brown tumors, and in secondary hyperparathyroidism (from renal disease), the characteristic "rugger jersey" spine.

## Types

- **Primary** — Parathyroid adenoma or hyperplasia; causes hypercalcemia with increased bone resorption
- **Secondary** — Response to chronic hypocalcemia (chronic kidney disease); causes rugger jersey spine and osteosclerosis
- **Tertiary** — Autonomous PTH secretion after prolonged secondary hyperparathyroidism

## Spine Imaging Findings

- **Osteopenia** — Generalized decreased bone density
- **Subchondral resorption** — Erosion of the vertebral endplates
- **Brown tumors** — Expansile lytic lesions containing hemorrhagic tissue and giant cells; rare in the spine
- **Rugger jersey spine** — In secondary hyperparathyroidism (see [Renal Osteodystrophy](renal-osteodystrophy.md))
- **Compression fractures** — From weakened bone

!!! tip "Clinical Pearl"
    Primary hyperparathyroidism produces diffuse osteopenia and subchondral resorption, while secondary hyperparathyroidism (renal) produces the rugger jersey spine with osteosclerosis. This distinction reflects the different pathophysiology — primary causes net bone loss, while secondary produces a mixture of resorption and reactive new bone formation.

## Key Points

- Primary hyperparathyroidism causes osteopenia and subchondral resorption
- Secondary hyperparathyroidism causes rugger jersey spine and osteosclerosis
- Brown tumors are expansile lytic lesions — rare in the spine
- Compression fractures occur from weakened bone
- Check serum calcium, phosphate, and PTH levels
