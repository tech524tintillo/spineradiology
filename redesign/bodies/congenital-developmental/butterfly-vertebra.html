Butterfly Vertebra¶

Definition¶

A butterfly vertebra is a congenital vertebral anomaly resulting from failure of fusion of the lateral chondrification centers of the vertebral body. This produces a sagittal cleft through the vertebral body, giving it a "butterfly" appearance on frontal (AP) radiograph or coronal CT. It is a benign incidental finding that requires no treatment.

Imaging Findings¶

Radiography¶

  • AP view — The vertebral body has a sagittal cleft, creating two lateral halves resembling butterfly wings. The endplates are concave toward the cleft.
  • Lateral view — Anterior notching or concavity of the vertebral body (the sagittal cleft is seen as an indentation)

CT¶

  • Coronal images — Sagittal cleft clearly demonstrated, with two hemivertebral bodies separated by a midline defect
  • Axial images — Midline sagittal cleft through the vertebral body

MRI¶

  • Not typically necessary for diagnosis
  • The cleft is visible on coronal images
  • Normal disc spaces above and below

Clinical Pearl

Butterfly vertebra is a benign incidental finding that can be confused with a vertebral body fracture (compression or burst) on lateral radiograph due to the anterior notching. The AP view resolves the diagnosis by showing the characteristic sagittal cleft. No follow-up or treatment is needed.

Key Points¶

  • Failure of fusion of lateral chondrification centers producing a sagittal cleft
  • Characteristic "butterfly" appearance on AP radiograph
  • Benign incidental finding — no treatment or follow-up needed
  • Anterior notching on lateral view can mimic fracture — AP view is diagnostic
  • Most common in the thoracic and lumbar spine
