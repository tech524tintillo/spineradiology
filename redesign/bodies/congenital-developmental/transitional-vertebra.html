Transitional Vertebra¶

Definition¶

A transitional vertebra (lumbosacral transitional vertebra, LSTV) is a congenital variant in which the lowest lumbar vertebra has characteristics of the sacrum (sacralization of L5) or the highest sacral segment has characteristics of a lumbar vertebra (lumbarization of S1). LSTVs are common, present in approximately 15–30% of the population.

Classification — Castellvi¶

  • Type I — Dysplastic (enlarged) transverse process (>19 mm). Unilateral (Ia) or bilateral (Ib).
  • Type II — Pseudoarticulation between the enlarged transverse process and the sacral ala, forming a diarthrodial joint. Unilateral (IIa) or bilateral (IIb).
  • Type III — Complete bony fusion of the transverse process to the sacral ala. Unilateral (IIIa) or bilateral (IIIb).
  • Type IV — Type II on one side and Type III on the other (asymmetric).

Clinical Significance¶

Bertolotti Syndrome¶

Low back pain attributed to a transitional vertebra — the pseudoarticulation (Type II) or the contralateral facet joint and disc can be a pain generator.

Numbering Errors¶

The most important clinical implication of LSTVs is vertebral numbering confusion — if a radiologist and surgeon count vertebral levels differently, the wrong disc may be operated on. Whole-spine or cervicothoracic scout images help establish accurate numbering.

Imaging Findings¶

Radiography¶

  • AP view — Enlarged, "winged" transverse process at L5 (or lumbarized S1) that may articulate with or fuse to the sacral ala
  • Unilateral or bilateral

CT¶

  • Best demonstrates the bony morphology — pseudoarticulation, fusion, or dysplastic transverse process
  • Helps with accurate vertebral numbering

MRI¶

  • The disc above a sacralized L5 is typically the last mobile disc and may show premature degeneration
  • The "last normal disc" sign — the lowest disc with a normal nucleus pulposus signal helps identify the transitional level
  • The most accurate method of numbering the vertebrae is to count down the vertebrae from C2 from the localizer sequences of the entire spine
  • The iliolumbar ligament usually arises from the transverse process of L5 and can also be used to base numbering

Clinical Pearl

The most important clinical consequence of a transitional vertebra is the risk of wrong-level surgery due to vertebral numbering confusion. A whole-spine localizer or correlation with a prior study that includes the cervicothoracic junction is essential for accurate surgical planning. When reporting an MRI with an LSTV, clearly state the numbering convention used.

Key Points¶

  • Present in 15–30% of the population — common variant
  • Castellvi classification based on transverse process morphology (I–IV)
  • Biggest clinical risk is wrong-level surgery from vertebral numbering errors
  • Whole-spine or cervicothoracic localizer ensures accurate numbering
  • Bertolotti syndrome — back pain from the pseudoarticulation or adjacent segment degeneration
