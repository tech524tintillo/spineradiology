Venous Congestion Myelopathy¶

Definition¶

Venous congestion myelopathy (Foix-Alajouanine syndrome in its most severe form) results from impaired venous drainage of the spinal cord, most commonly caused by a spinal dural arteriovenous fistula. The elevated venous pressure reduces the arteriovenous pressure gradient, causing cord edema, ischemia, and progressive neurological deterioration.

Etiology¶

  • Spinal dural arteriovenous fistula — The most common cause
  • Perimedullary arteriovenous fistula
  • Spinal venous thrombosis — Rare
  • Epidural venous compression — From tumors or other mass lesions

Imaging Findings¶

MRI¶

  • Diffuse cord T2 hyperintensity — Extending over multiple segments, typically centromedullary, most prominent in the lower thoracic cord and conus
  • Flow voids on the cord surface — Dilated perimedullary veins from the underlying vascular malformation
  • Cord expansion — Mild, from edema
  • Enhancement — Patchy cord enhancement from breakdown of the blood-cord barrier
  • Conus involvement — Commonly affected

Clinical Pearl

Venous congestion myelopathy is a treatable cause of progressive paraplegia. The MRI pattern of diffuse cord edema with perimedullary flow voids should trigger urgent evaluation for a dural AVF with spinal angiography. Early treatment (embolization or surgical disconnection) can halt progression and allow recovery, while delayed diagnosis leads to irreversible cord damage.

Key Points¶

  • Most commonly caused by spinal dural AVF
  • Diffuse cord T2 hyperintensity with perimedullary flow voids is the classic MRI pattern
  • Treatable if diagnosed early — irreversible if delayed
  • Spinal angiography confirms the underlying vascular malformation
  • Foix-Alajouanine syndrome is the severe, end-stage manifestation with cord necrosis
