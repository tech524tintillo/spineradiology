Perimedullary AVF¶

Definition¶

A perimedullary arteriovenous fistula (Type IV) is a direct arterial-venous connection on the pial surface of the spinal cord, without an intervening nidus. The anterior spinal artery or posterior spinal arteries directly shunt into a perimedullary vein. Three subtypes exist based on size and flow characteristics.

Subtypes¶

  • Type IVa — Small, single feeder, low flow. May be clinically silent or cause mild myelopathy.
  • Type IVb — Medium, multiple feeders, moderate flow. Progressive myelopathy.
  • Type IVc — Large, giant perimedullary vein, high flow. Can cause mass effect from the giant venous varix in addition to venous congestion.

Imaging Findings¶

MRI¶

  • Dilated perimedullary veins (flow voids on the cord surface)
  • Cord edema from venous congestion (similar to dural AVF)
  • Type IVc may show a large venous varix (round flow void) that can compress the cord
  • MRI appearance can overlap with dural AVF

Spinal Angiography¶

  • Direct connection from anterior or posterior spinal artery to perimedullary vein on the cord surface
  • No nidus (distinguishes from AVM)
  • The fistula is on the cord surface (distinguishes from dural AVF, which is at the nerve root sleeve)

Clinical Pearl

Perimedullary AVF can be difficult to distinguish from dural AVF on MRI alone — both show cord edema and perimedullary flow voids. Spinal angiography is required to distinguish them because the treatment approaches differ: dural AVFs are treated at the dural sleeve level, while perimedullary AVFs require treatment at the pial surface of the cord.

Key Points¶

  • Direct fistula on the pial surface of the cord — no nidus
  • Three subtypes by size and flow (IVa, IVb, IVc)
  • MRI overlaps with dural AVF — angiography distinguishes them
  • Type IVc can cause cord compression from giant venous varix
  • Treatment may include embolization and/or surgery
