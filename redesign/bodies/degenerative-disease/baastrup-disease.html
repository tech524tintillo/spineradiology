Baastrup Disease¶

Definition¶

Baastrup disease (also called "kissing spines") is a condition in which adjacent spinous processes make contact and develop degenerative changes — including sclerosis, flattening, bursitis, and osteophyte formation — at their points of contact. It results from loss of disc height and lumbar hyperextension.

Pathophysiology¶

  • As disc height decreases, the interspinous space narrows
  • Adjacent spinous processes approximate and eventually contact each other
  • Repeated contact causes reactive sclerosis, cortical flattening, and sometimes a neobursa (interspinous bursa) between the spinous processes
  • Most common in the lumbar spine (L4–L5, L3–L4) where the spinous processes are large and lordosis brings them closer together

Imaging Findings¶

Radiography¶

  • Sclerosis and flattening of adjacent spinous process tips
  • Close approximation or contact between spinous processes
  • Best seen on the lateral view

CT¶

  • Sclerosis, flattening, and enlargement of opposing spinous process surfaces
  • May show cyst formation between the spinous processes

MRI¶

Finding Appearance
Interspinous bursitis T2-bright fluid signal between the sclerotic spinous processes
Marrow edema T2/STIR hyperintensity within the opposing spinous processes
Sclerosis Low T1 and T2 signal at the spinous process tips
Epidural extension Rarely, the interspinous bursa can extend anteriorly into the posterior epidural space, mimicking an epidural cyst

Clinical Pearl

Baastrup disease can be a source of midline low back pain that is often overlooked. It is typically worse with extension (which brings the spinous processes closer together) and relieved with flexion. When a patient has midline tenderness and extension-provoked pain, look carefully at the interspinous spaces on sagittal MRI for T2-bright fluid signal and spinous process edema. An interspinous injection (steroid + anesthetic) under fluoroscopy can be both diagnostic and therapeutic.

Key Points¶

  • "Kissing spines" — adjacent spinous processes contact each other due to disc height loss
  • Most common in the lumbar spine at L3–L5
  • MRI shows interspinous bursitis (T2-bright fluid) and spinous process edema
  • An often-overlooked cause of midline, extension-provoked low back pain
  • Interspinous injection can be diagnostic and therapeutic
