Ossification of the Posterior Longitudinal Ligament (OPLL)¶

Definition¶

Ossification of the posterior longitudinal ligament (OPLL) is a condition in which the posterior longitudinal ligament undergoes ectopic bone formation within the vertebral canal, resulting in narrowing of the spinal canal and potential compression of the spinal cord or nerve roots. It is most common in the cervical spine and in East Asian populations.

Epidemiology¶

  • Prevalence: 2–4% in Japan; 0.1–1.7% in Western populations
  • Male predominance (2:1)
  • Mean age of presentation: 50–60 years
  • Most common location: cervical spine (C2–C5), followed by thoracic spine
  • Associated with DISH (~25% of OPLL patients also have DISH), diabetes, and metabolic syndrome

Classification (Morphologic Types on CT)¶

Type Description Canal Narrowing
Continuous Ossification spans multiple vertebral bodies as a long, continuous bar Most severe
Segmental Ossification behind individual vertebral bodies with gaps at disc levels Moderate
Mixed Combination of continuous and segmental Variable
Circumscribed Focal ossification behind one or two vertebral segments Least severe

Imaging Findings¶

CT — Gold Standard for Characterization¶

  • Hyperdense ossification posterior to the vertebral bodies within the spinal canal
  • Best evaluated on sagittal and axial reformats
  • Measure: AP thickness of OPLL, percent canal occupation, and remaining canal diameter
  • Occupying ratio (OPLL thickness / AP canal diameter × 100): >60% strongly associated with myelopathy
  • CT is superior to MRI for delineating the extent and morphology of ossification

MRI¶

Finding Appearance
OPLL mass Low signal on all sequences (ossified bone); may have variable signal if immature ossification
Cord compression Cord deformation, flattening against the OPLL mass
Myelopathy T2 hyperintensity within the cord at the level of maximal compression
Myelomalacia T1 hypointensity within the cord — indicates irreversible damage

Radiography¶

  • Opaque strip posterior to the vertebral bodies on the lateral view
  • Subtle and easily missed — CT is far more sensitive

Clinical Pearl

OPLL presents a unique surgical challenge: anterior approaches (ACDF, corpectomy) risk dural tears because the ossified ligament is often adherent to or even fused with the dura. Posterior approaches (laminoplasty, laminectomy) avoid the ossified mass but may be less effective for severe anterior compression. The surgical approach depends on the number of levels involved, the morphologic type, and the degree of kyphosis. CT is essential for preoperative planning — it defines the OPLL morphology and identifies dural ossification (which predicts high risk of CSF leak with anterior surgery).

Key Points¶

  • OPLL is ectopic ossification of the PLL within the spinal canal — most common in the cervical spine
  • Higher prevalence in East Asian populations; associated with DISH and diabetes
  • CT is the gold standard for morphologic classification and surgical planning
  • Occupying ratio >60% strongly predicts myelopathy
  • MRI is essential for evaluating cord compression and signal change
  • Surgery is complex — approach depends on OPLL morphology and extent
