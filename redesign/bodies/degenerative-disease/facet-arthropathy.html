Facet Arthropathy¶

Definition¶

Facet arthropathy (facet joint osteoarthritis) is the degenerative deterioration of the facet (zygapophyseal) joints, characterized by cartilage loss, joint space narrowing, subchondral sclerosis, osteophyte formation, and joint effusion. It is a major contributor of back/neck pain and spinal stenosis.

Pathophysiology¶

  • Disc degeneration and height loss increase loading on the facet joints
  • Progressive cartilage erosion leads to bone-on-bone contact
  • Osteophyte formation and capsular hypertrophy narrow the lateral recess and foramen
  • Synovial cysts may develop from the degenerated joint

Imaging Findings¶

CT — Best for Bony Detail¶

  • Joint space narrowing
  • Subchondral sclerosis and cysts
  • Osteophyte formation from articular processes
  • Vacuum phenomenon within the facet joint
  • Facet hypertrophy narrowing the lateral recess or foramen

MRI¶

Finding Appearance
Joint effusion T2-bright fluid within the facet joint (>1 mm)
Cartilage loss Narrowed joint space
Periarticular edema T2/STIR hyperintensity around the joint (active inflammation)
Synovial cyst T2-bright, well-defined cyst arising from the joint; may have low-signal rim
Facet hypertrophy Enlarged articular processes encroaching on lateral recess or foramen

Grading (Pathria Classification)¶

Grade Description
0 Normal
1 Mild — joint space narrowing, small osteophytes
2 Moderate — sclerosis, moderate osteophytes, moderate narrowing
3 Severe — marked osteophytes, severe narrowing, subchondral cysts

Clinical Pearl

Facet arthropathy is a common cause of axial (non-radicular) low back pain, but diagnosing it as the pain source is challenging because facet degeneration is nearly universal with aging and is often asymptomatic. Facet joint effusion on MRI and bone marrow edema adjacent to the facet on STIR are the best imaging markers for an actively symptomatic facet joint. These findings can help target diagnostic medial branch blocks, which, if positive, can lead to radiofrequency ablation for longer-lasting pain relief.

Key Points¶

  • Facet arthropathy is facet joint osteoarthritis — cartilage loss, sclerosis, osteophytes, effusion
  • A major contributor to lateral recess and foraminal stenosis
  • CT is best for bony changes; MRI for effusion, edema, and synovial cysts
  • Facet effusion and periarticular edema on MRI suggest active/symptomatic disease
  • Diagnosis of facet-mediated pain requires clinical correlation and often diagnostic blocks
  • Synovial cysts from degenerated facets can cause radiculopathy
