Chondrosarcoma¶

Definition¶

Chondrosarcoma is a malignant cartilage-forming tumor that rarely affects the spine (approximately 7–12% of all chondrosarcomas). It typically involves the vertebral body or posterior elements and is characterized by chondroid (cartilage) matrix mineralization. It is usually low to intermediate grade and slow-growing.

Imaging Findings¶

CT¶

  • Lytic, destructive mass with chondroid matrix — characteristic "rings and arcs" or "popcorn" pattern of calcification
  • Cortical destruction and soft tissue extension
  • Endosteal scalloping
  • The chondroid matrix pattern is the key diagnostic feature

MRI¶

  • Very high T2 signal — Characteristic of cartilage tumors (lobulated, bright T2 signal resembling hyaline cartilage)
  • Low to intermediate T1 signal
  • Peripheral and septal "rings and arcs" enhancement pattern (enhancing septa around non-enhancing cartilage lobules)
  • Epidural extension may cause cord compression

Clinical Pearl

The combination of a destructive spinal mass with "rings and arcs" chondroid calcification on CT and very high lobulated T2 signal on MRI is virtually diagnostic of a cartilage tumor (enchondroma or chondrosarcoma). In the spine, chondrosarcoma is far more common than enchondroma.

Management¶

  • En bloc surgical resection with wide margins is the treatment of choice
  • Chondrosarcomas are resistant to conventional chemotherapy and radiation
  • Proton beam therapy may be used for unresectable or residual disease
  • Recurrence is common with inadequate margins

Key Points¶

  • Chondrosarcoma produces chondroid matrix ("rings and arcs" calcification on CT)
  • Very high T2 signal on MRI reflects cartilage content
  • The spine is an uncommon but recognized location
  • Resistant to chemotherapy and radiation — surgery is the primary treatment
  • Distinguish from chordoma (midline, no chondroid matrix) and osteosarcoma (osteoid matrix)
