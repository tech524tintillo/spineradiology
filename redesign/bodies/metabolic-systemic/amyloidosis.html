Amyloidosis¶

Overview¶

Amyloid deposition in the spine can cause destructive spondyloarthropathy, most commonly in patients on long-term hemodialysis (beta-2 microglobulin amyloidosis). It can also occur in systemic AL amyloidosis. The imaging findings mimic infection or tumor, making clinical context essential for diagnosis.

Imaging Findings¶

  • Disc space narrowing with endplate erosions
  • Vertebral body destruction
  • Perivertebral soft tissue mass
  • No significant inflammatory response (unlike infection)
  • May produce spinal stenosis from ligamentous and soft tissue deposition
  • Facet joint and ligamentum flavum thickening

Distinguishing Features¶

  • Clinical context: long-term hemodialysis patient
  • Normal inflammatory markers (unlike infection)
  • No disc T2 hyperintensity (unlike pyogenic spondylodiscitis)
  • Biopsy with Congo red staining confirms: apple-green birefringence under polarized light

Clinical Pearl

In a hemodialysis patient with destructive spondyloarthropathy and normal inflammatory markers, consider beta-2 microglobulin amyloidosis. The imaging can mimic infection, but the absence of fever, normal CRP/ESR, and dialysis history are key clues. CT-guided biopsy with Congo red staining confirms the diagnosis.

Key Points¶

  • Most common in long-term hemodialysis patients (beta-2 microglobulin)
  • Destructive spondyloarthropathy mimicking infection or tumor
  • Normal inflammatory markers help distinguish from infection
  • Congo red staining of biopsy tissue is diagnostic
  • Facet and ligamentum flavum thickening can cause spinal stenosis
